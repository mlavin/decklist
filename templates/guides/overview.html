Card and Deck Guide

Overview

Decks for the Pokémon Trading Card Game are made of 60 cards in total. These cards are a mixture of different types of cards including Pokémon, Trainer (Supporter, Item, Tool, Stadium), and Energy cards. Roughly these break down into:

Pokémon
8-20
Items
12-20
Supporters
9-15
Stadiums
0-4
Energy
8-15

These ranges vary depending on the overall strategy of the deck and some decks may require cards outside of these general ranges. It should be noted that other than basic energy, you cannot have more than four of the same card with the same name in a deck.

Formats

Competitive Pokémon play is broken into two formats which dictate the set of cards which are allowable for play. These formats are Standard which includes the cards from Primal Clash (released Feb 2015) through the Sun and Moon base set (released Feb 2017) and Expanded which includes the cards from the Black and White base set (released April 2011) through the Sun and Moon base set (released Feb 2017). These formats rotate each September, dropping 3-5 of the oldest sets. Cards from older sets which have since been reprinted in legal set are still legal for play in that format as long as the wording on the card has not changed.

For example, the popular supporter N has been reprinted a number of times. While the original sets Noble Victories (released Nov 2011) and Dark Explorers (released May 2012) are no longer legal in Standard play, you can play those prints of N because they have the same wording at N when it was reprinted in Fates Collide (released May 2016) which is still legal in Standard.

For casual play, you may not care about whether cards are legal for tournament play, but it's worth keeping in mind that these cards are printed together and rotated to balance the game. It's also worth knowing that there might be other prints of the same card which could be cheaper or easier to find. This guide is written using examples from the Standard format but the principles apply to all formats. With larger formats such as Expanded there are more cards to choose from and more options for filling the roles needed to build a balanced and consitent deck.

Deck Strategy and Consistency

Decks are built around a particular strategy (or strategies) which will allow the player to win the game whether that is by taking their six prizes first or by discarding all of their oppenent's cards so that they cannot continue the game. Some decks do this through big, aggressive attacks which knock out most other Pokémon in a single hit. Other decks plan to consistently hit for two-hit knockouts. Some decks try to stop opponent's attacks from dealing much sometimes even any damage. Others try to deny their opponent any energy so they simply cannot attack. Whatever it is, a deck should have a clear strategy and the cards included in the deck should work to compliment that goal. Any card game involves a bit of randomness so decks are called consistent when they can effectively work towards their strategy regardless of the randomness of the draw.

Deck Space

When you see a list of sixty cards which make up a deck it's important to understand that many of the cards can be flexible in how many are used or used at all. When you understand the role of certain cards in a deck you can better understand which cards might be reduced or removed and other cards that might take their place to fill a similar role or trade off for cost or work better in certain match-ups. When people talk about making "space" in a list, they mean deciding which cards to remove to put in another card they want. In the end there is rarely a perfect list and as new cards come out there are more choices and trade-offs to make.

Cards Roles

The purpose of each card in a deck roughly fits into one of the below categories. Read on for each role guide to find out more and see some of the most common cards used for these roles.

Attackers

Attacking with your Pokémon to knock out your opponent's Pokémon to take your six prizes is the most common way to win the game so it shouldn't be surprising that most decks are built around a single attacking Pokémon or a few complimentary Pokémon. Read our attacking Pokémon guide to find our more about what makes a good choice for an attacking Pokémon.

Supporting Pokémon

Many decks include Pokémon only for their abilities rather than their attacks. These abilities can be used to either help with one of the other roles such as energy acceleration, draw support, or disruption. The supporting Pokémon guide goes through common supporting cards which can fit into your deck.

Deck Search

To get over the randomness of the draw, decks need to contain cards which help for exactly what you need. That might include searching for Pokémon, energy, items, supporters, or stadiums. Sometimes these cards require discarding something that you have for something that you need but that will be worth it to get the right card for the right situation. The deck search guide will breakdown some of the best cards for finding things in your deck.

Draw Support

Your deck is sixty cards and starting with seven you draw one per turn. That will take a lot of turns to get through most of your cards. Each with cards to search out one or two that you might need, there are many times to need more. Draw support cards help you get you get a fresh new hand and get your deck setup.

Energy Acceleration

The rules only allow for attaching one engery per turn. To use attacks costing more than one energy quickly or to power up a second attacker on the bench, most decks need some form of energy acceleration. This can come in the form of trainers or Pokémon attacks or abilities but the amount of support and types of cards you'll need vary based on the type of energy you need. Find out some of the common forms of energy acceleration in our guide.

Healing

Healing can be more of a nice to have than a requiement in most desks. At the same time, a well timed heal on your main attacker can cause your oppentent to need an additional turn to get the knockout. If you can use that extra turn to take a knockout of your own or setup your next attack, it could swing the game in your favor.

Recycle

You'll worry less about discarding cards early in the game if you know you have a way to recover them later if needed. Whether it's energy, Pokémon, or trainer cards, it helps decks to have the ability to use the same card more than once in a game. Having some amount of recycle in a deck can sometimes be the difference between winning and decking out.

Disruption

You have your plan and your opponent has theirs. While you are playing cards to help setup your own gameplan, it can help to play some cards to disrupt your opponent as well. That might mean discarding their energy or changing their active Pokémon. Particularly if you need more time to get setup, buying an extra turn might just be what your deck is missing. In fact some decks are built entirely around disruption

Switching

With the inclusion of your supporting Pokémon, you might not always start the Pokémon that you want. In other situations, your oppenent might change your active Pokémon. Since energy is a precious resource in the game you don't want to pay retreat if you don't have to so having a plan on switching your Pokémon is needed in every deck. There are items to help with switching as well as tools, stadiums, and other Pokémon and you might want a mix of these switching cards for different situations.

Stadiums

Stadiums can fit into one of the previous roles or can work to boost your deck strategy in other ways. Most decks want to include a stadium or two if for no other reason than to discard their opponents stadium and to disrupt their strategy.
